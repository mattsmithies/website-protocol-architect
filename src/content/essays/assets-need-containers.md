Before the shipping container, global trade existed but it was slow, fragile, and expensive. Every port had its own loading process, its own dimensions, its own handling assumptions. The shipping container did not change what was being shipped. It changed the meta structure around it — and unlocked an entirely different scale of coordination.

Digital assets have the same problem. Tokens, credentials, carbon credits, data rights — the assets exist, but each system defines them differently. There is no shared container. No common envelope that makes an asset searchable across registries, indexable across platforms, and comparable across markets. Without this, every integration is bespoke, every marketplace is a silo, and every audit requires translation.

The systems that will define the next era of digital assets are not the ones that issue the most tokens. They are the ones that solve the meta structure problem — the standardised envelope that lets any asset be discovered, compared, verified, and moved without losing its provenance or meaning. The shipping container did not win because it was innovative. It won because it was boring, universal, and correct. Asset infrastructure needs the same discipline.
